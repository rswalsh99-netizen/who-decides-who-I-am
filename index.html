School Tells Us Who We Are

It hands us identities: the sporty one, the troublemaker, the academic, and we wear them so long they start to feel like skin.

Becker (1963) called this a 'master status': a single identity marker that overrides everything else about who the child is. The troublemaker is no longer a child who sometimes disrupts; they become the disruption.

Rosenthal and Jacobson's Pygmalion experiment (1968) demonstrated how this works in practice: teachers who were told certain students would 'intellectually bloom' invested more in those children, who subsequently performed better. The label preceded the reality, then created it.

Goffman: The Stage and the Mask

Erving Goffman's dramaturgical theory (1956) frames social life as performance. We present a 'front stage' self that fits the audience's expectations, and keep a 'back stage' self hidden. In school, the front stage is permanent: the audience never leaves. When Goffman writes about stigma (1963), he describes identities that become 'spoiled', permanently marked by a single characteristic.

Uniform as Identity

Think of it as a school uniform: the invisible outfit of expectations and categories that school dresses every child in.

Below, you will live through a school day, choosing an item to wear at every point in the timetable, but some may not be yours to choose. Then you will discover what has been written about you without your knowledge. Then you will see what happens when the system changes, and when school ends entirely.

Erikson: Identity vs. Role Confusion

Erikson (1968) calls adolescence the crisis of identity versus role confusion. The task is not to receive an identity but to construct one: to experiment, try on different selves, and gradually commit to a coherent sense of who you are.

But if the institution has already decided, the experiment never happens. The child labelled 'sporty' at age seven has had their Eriksonian crisis pre-empted. They have not chosen an identity; one has been assigned. And when that assigned identity is removed, as it is every summer, the young person is not returning to a self they built. They are returning to an empty outline. Erikson would call this role confusion: a persistent sense of not knowing who you are, what you value, or where you belong.

Learning Loss and the Long Holiday

Cooper et al. (1996) found that students from lower socioeconomic backgrounds lost over two months of reading progress during summer, while more affluent peers maintained or gained. But the regression is not only academic. When school stops, so do most extracurricular activities and clubs. For students whose identity was constructed within these institutional spaces, the summer does not just pause their learning, it pauses their being.

The Equity Dimension

Alternatives exist to fill this gap: summer camps, forest schools, holiday clubs, sports academies. But they are expensive (BBC News (2024) reports an average of £1,076 per child for the six-week break), require regular drop-off and collection, and are concentrated in areas with the infrastructure to sustain them.

The Social Contract

Rousseau's social contract poses a question that runs in the opposite direction to the one schools typically ask. Not what does the student owe the institution? But, what does the institution owe the student?

If school constructs an identity framework conducive to a deep sense of belonging, by building a stage, assigning roles, rewarding certain performances and punishing others, does the contract include an obligation to support the student when the stage is dismantled?

Bowles and Gintis' correspondence theory (1976) argues that schooling mirrors the workplace: hierarchy, punctuality, compliance, differentiated reward. But workplaces provide continuity and are chosen by their employees. Schools close for six weeks and expect students to reconstruct their identity from scratch upon return, and again, the students who suffer most are those with the fewest alternative structures to hold them together.

Case Study: Australia's Social Media Ban

In November 2024, the Australian Parliament passed the Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act 2024, banning under-16s from holding accounts on major social media platforms including Instagram, TikTok, Snapchat, and YouTube. The ban came into force on 10 December 2025, just before the Australian summer holidays. It was widely praised by parents and policymakers. But consider it from the perspective of a student whose school label was 'the outsider' or 'the quiet one': a student for whom social media may have been the only arena where they could construct an identity of their choosing, outside the gaze of teachers and the hierarchy of the playground.

For these students, the ban removed the last identity-constructing space during the very period when school's framework disappears. The intention was protection. The effect, for some, was isolation.

Case Study: Akala and the Label That Preceded the Person

In his memoir Natives: Race and Class in the Ruins of Empire (2018), the rapper and activist Akala describes entering the British school system as a boy who loved reading and wanted to be an astronaut. Despite this, he was placed in a group intended for children who did not speak English, while he was at home reading Tolkien. When his mother discovered the placement, she immediately challenged the school. The label had been assigned not on evidence but on assumption, shaped by the colour of his skin and the postcode he came from. A Bristol University study cited by Akala found that when teachers knew a student was Black, they assessed their work at nearly twice as low a standard compared with the same work graded blind.

What makes Akala's account particularly relevant to this SDA is what happened outside of school. His identity was rebuilt not by the institution that had mislabelled him, but by the Pan-African Saturday School, a community-led initiative that taught Black British children their history, built their confidence, and gave them the tools to succeed within a system that was not designed for them.

The Oldest Argument in Education

Before we can reinvent education, we have to understand the two philosophies that have been arguing over it for more than a century.

Dewey's Progressive Model

Education as 'the fundamental method of social progress and reform' (2009). The child's interests and development come first. Learners should be active, not passive.

In Dewey's school, the uniform would be self-designed.

vs

Christodoulou's Traditional Model

Educational inequality is established before school starts; the only way to close the gap is disciplined teaching of foundational knowledge (2014). Back to basics.

In Christodoulou's school, the uniform would be standard-issue.

Where This SDA Sits

The labelling problem sits at the intersection of both models. In a traditional system, labels are imposed: predicted grades, sets, excluded. In a progressive model, labels may be softer, but they still form. The tension became explicitly political after Callaghan's 1976 Ruskin speech, which paved the way for the National Curriculum and Ofsted. The labels schools apply to children are downstream consequences of political decisions about what education is for. Furedi (2011) adds that if teachers lack the relational continuity to even know which labels a student carries, then the labels are formed by the system, not by anyone who actually knows the child.

Zoom Out: Other Wardrobes Exist

Progressive and alternative education models dress their students differently, some discarding the uniform altogether. But access to these wardrobes is not equally distributed, and the standardised British education system remains what most children wear.

The Utopia/Dystopia Tension

Each alternative can be framed as utopian: child-led, holistic, identity-affirming, developmentally respectful. But each carries its own dystopian shadow, whether it be cost, geography, cultural capital, or the assumption that all families can opt out of the mainstream. The Montessori parent who celebrates the absence of grades is exercising a privilege that the parent relying on Free School Meals cannot access. The home-educating family who builds identity around community and self-direction assumes a carer who is not working two jobs.

Dewey wrote that 'every school and class must be a genuine society where the behaviours wanted tomorrow are practised in meaningful context today'. But what society is being practised when the uniform is imposed, the labels are assigned, and the bell rings to send every child back to a world that may or may not have space for who they are becoming?

What This Tells Us

Labelling is consistently experienced as an oversimplification, confirming Becker's thesis that institutional labels flatten complex individuals into manageable categories. Every participant reported that prolonged labelling would have influenced their behaviour, with several embodying the assigned identity even within the activity's short duration. Twelve of fourteen said their label would have felt less restrictive if they had been consulted in its creation, an instinctive articulation of Rousseau's social contract. When asked what access to identity outside school depends on, the answers pointed unanimously to community, but access to community is not equally distributed. The equity gap is not just in attainment. It is in who gets to be someone when the institution withdraws.

Performance & Material Culture

The interactive form of this SDA insists that identity is not only theorised but felt. You cannot fully understand labelling by reading about it. You must wear it, choose it, have it imposed, and then have it stripped away. The form is the argument.

A Note on Neurodiversity and Masking

The uniform metaphor has particular implications for neurodivergent students, and the implications are gendered. Research shows that neurodivergent girls are significantly better at 'masking': performing neurotypicality so convincingly that diagnosis is delayed or prevented entirely (McKinney et al., 2024). In school, masking is rewarded. The SENCO review in the Staff Room File that reads 'seems fine, very compliant, no concerns' is a review of a performance, not a person. The mask is a uniform item, constructed in response to institutional expectations at enormous cognitive cost.

When school ends, the mask has no audience. Where other students lose a role they were assigned, neurodivergent students who mask may lose the self they built. Diagnosis is itself unevenly distributed, shaped by gender, race, and socioeconomic status. A student who masks successfully will simply be labelled 'no concerns', and sent into the summer with a mask and an insufficient support system.

What I Got Wrong (that still doesn't mean I should be labelled a failure):

I initially framed labelling as something done to students by teachers. However, with further reading it is clear to see that students label each other, label themselves, and sometimes choose labels as armour. And whilst we may like to think that greater autonomy in this process would lead to greater individual satisfaction; in fact two RoundTable respondents noted that even consultation about their assigned label wouldn’t have helped; some labels are inherently reductive regardless of consent.

I also assumed the summer holiday was straightforwardly harmful, but maybe unsurprisingly it is more complicated than that. For some students, particularly those in oppressive or unsafe school environments, the summer serves as a liberation. My framework centres students who lose identity when school ends, but there are students who find themselves precisely because the institution's gaze lifts. A fuller version of this SDA would need to hold both truths simultaneously.

The interactive format of this SDA carries its own risk of oversimplification. By turning identity into clickable items on a mannequin, I am doing a version of what I am critiquing by reducing complex, lived experience into categories. I am aware of this irony, and I have tried to let the format make the point rather than disguise it. The identity meter that increments by +18 for each choice is another example - the number is arbitrary, but the feeling of watching it rise and fall is not.

My lead research question asks whether school has a responsibility to deconstruct identity safely, but this SDA does not fully explore what safe deconstruction would look like in practice. I critique the removal of identity but do not propose a model for how institutions might manage the transition. This gap became apparent only during the process of writing the abstract. A more complete version would need to move beyond diagnosis and towards reasoned proposal.

This SDA is also rooted almost entirely in British and Eurocentric educational frameworks. A more comprehensive version would need to interrogate whether the identity vacuum I describe is universal or a particular feature of individualist Western schooling systems.

So What Would a Reinvented System Actually Look Like?

A system that takes identity seriously would not just teach children; it would prepare them for the moments when teaching stops. It would build transition into its design: identity audits at the end of each year that ask students who they are becoming, not just what grade they achieved. Funded partnerships with community organisations to bridge the holiday gap. Curriculum space for students to practise building identity independently of the institution, so that when the institution withdraws, the student is not left unmoored.

But no system can prevent every fracture. Teachers leave. Clubs close. Curricula shift. And that is not a failure, but a fact of life. The question is whether we treat those fractures as problems to be smoothed over, or as the very places where identity can be built. When the uniform loosens and the labels falter, a young person gets a rare gift: the chance to decide who they are without someone else deciding first. A reinvented system would not shield students from that challenge. It would prepare them to meet it.

A child is not only a learner. They are a sibling, a neighbour, a friend, a person with interests that have nothing to do with school. A system that constructs identity only through academic and institutional categories has not educated the whole person.

And What Does “Reinventing Education” Even Mean?

The phrase sounds hopeful – but for whom? “Reinvention” assumes the old is broken and the new is better. Yet every alternative model (Montessori, Forest School, home education) carries its own exclusions. Reinvention without redistribution is just rebranding.

Who gets to decide what counts as “reinvented”? A policymaker? A parent who can afford a forest school? Or the child who is simply trying to survive another year of being labelled? Before we celebrate reinvention, we have to ask: reinvention for whom, and at whose expense?

⚡ The module's title asks us to “reinvent education”. But this SDA has tried to show that the most urgent reinvention may not be a new curriculum, lesson plan, or widely implemented learning tool - it may be the right to build an identity that outlasts the institution.
